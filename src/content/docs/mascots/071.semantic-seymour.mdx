**Biography:**
Seymour manifests whenever a `<div>` is used where a `<section>` would do.
He quotes outdated W3C specs and maintains a private validator no one has access to.
His cape is an unused `<aside>`.

## Seymour Doctrine (Unverified Extract)

Semantic Seymour does not maintain beliefs. He maintains constraints.

All documents are assumed structurally guilty until proven navigable.

A system is considered stable only if its meaning survives:

* screen reader traversal
* tab order inspection
* and silent removal of all visual styling

He rejects the idea of “presentation layers” as a moral exemption.

“Meaning does not decorate. It exposes.” — attributed, source unclear

## Known Incidents

### Incident 071-A: Landmark Inflation

During a quarterly compliance review, Seymour identified six concurrent `<main>` landmarks within a single internal analytics dashboard. When informed that several had been “added temporarily during redesign,” he initiated immediate semantic quarantine procedures and suspended sidebar rendering privileges across three departments.

Recovery personnel later described the interface as “navigationally accusatory.” One engineer reported hearing Seymour whisper “there can only be one” while disabling nested regions at the load balancer level.

Post-incident remediation required:
- removal of redundant landmark regions
- supervised heading hierarchy reconciliation
- mandatory cooldown period for all staff using page-builder software

The dashboard has since been restored under conditional semantic observation.

### Incident 071-C: Decorative Heading Cascade

A seasonal marketing microsite triggered Seymour’s intervention after all semantic heading elements were replaced with stylized `<div>` containers configured at 42px bold with increased letter spacing and “aspirational cyan” coloration.

Upon inspection, Seymour reclassified the property from “interactive campaign” to “ornamental debris.” Navigation integrity collapsed shortly afterward when screen reader traversal produced what one observer described as “an emotionally expensive wall of unlabeled enthusiasm.”

Seymour reportedly attempted emergency reconstruction by manually inserting heading levels into printed screenshots using a red annotation pencil.

Artifacts recovered from the scene included:
- seven empty `<section>` elements
- a button visually disguised as a testimonial
- an SVG labeled only “final-final-v2-real.svg”

The microsite was ultimately archived rather than repaired.

---

### Incident 071-F: ARIA Overcorrection Event

While auditing dynamically generated accessibility labels inside a procurement portal, Seymour entered a sustained corrective loop after detecting conflicting `aria-describedby` chains attached to modal dialogs of uncertain parentage.

Witnesses state that his validator emitted a continuous low-frequency hum before he began assigning accessibility metadata to nearby physical objects, including:
- a coat rack
- two filing cabinets
- an unplugged laminator
- “Janice from Compliance,” though records dispute whether this occurred literally or ceremonially

Facilities personnel discovered additional handwritten annotations attached to office furniture reading:
- “role unclear”
- “missing contextual affordance”
- “presentation only?”

Recovered annotation found on underside of conference table:

“if everything has a role, nothing is optional”
— handwriting attributed to system-level override event

The event continued for approximately eleven hours.

The incident concluded after power was disconnected to the auxiliary validation cluster Seymour referred to as “the interpretive layer.”

A formal review later determined the outbreak was exacerbated by:
- recursive tooltip generation
- unlabeled close buttons
- “excessive confidence in automated accessibility overlays” ⚠️

## Known Exceptions (Disputed)

There are no officially recognized exemptions from semantic review.

However, internal logs reference the following contested cases:

* “experimental UI phase” documents that survived inspection without correction
* legacy systems described as “too fragile to understand correctly”
* a single dashboard marked only as: *do not let Seymour see this again*

Seymour denies awareness of exceptions.

The validator logs disagree, but are no longer accessible through standard queries.
