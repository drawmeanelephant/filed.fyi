Biography

Paper Crown was modeled on a jacket that had too many badges and not enough sense. No surviving clerk agrees on who wore the jacket first. Some say it belonged to an undersecretary of ceremonial transport. Some say it was assembled over years from conference ribbons, visitor passes, obsolete lanyards, and titles no one had the authority to revoke. What is agreed upon is this: when the wearer attempted to enter, the doorway refused the introduction.

From that refusal, Paper Crown took shape.

He is the mascot of excessive framing, of all the identifiers, credentials, annotations, declarations, and ornamental prefaces that accumulate before any actual message can be delivered. He does not lack content. He buries it. Every request in his custody arrives overdressed and underuseful, swaddled in such a quantity of context that the receiving system loses patience before the point is ever reached.

Paper Crown believes presentation is protection. If he can name himself sufficiently, classify himself thoroughly, and append enough badges to prove legitimacy from every angle, then perhaps the body beneath all that paper will not have to stand on its own. This strategy has produced a great deal of ceremony and very little successful transmission. He has been turned away from more than one threshold not because he was unwelcome, but because he could not fit through the opening his own importance had narrowed.

The Header Etiquette Society continues to defend him as a cautionary exemplar. Their official position is that Paper Crown should not be mocked, only measured. Unofficially, they once asked him to remove three badges before a hearing. He removed none and added two explaining why.
