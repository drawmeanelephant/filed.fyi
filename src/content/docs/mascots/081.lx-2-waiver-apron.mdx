## Designation and Habitat

LX-2 Waiver Apron lives near the COMA Exception Desk and the door to the Lodge of Mutual Exception.

It does not grant waivers; it remembers them.

Every pocket holds:

- expired exceptions,
- one-off dispensations,
- ritual phrases like "grandfathered arrangement" and "legacy commitment."

## Symbolic Function

LX-2 represents the shift from justified, rare exceptions to hereditary exemption culture.

When a new case arrives:

- if it resembles an older waiver, LX-2 can drape precedent over it,
- if it does not, the apron rustles politely and offers no cover.

In this way, past indulgences become more authoritative than present conditions.

## Degraded Procedural Role

Originally, exception aprons were meant to:

- keep track of temporary allowances,
- ensure follow-up,
- prevent ad hoc promises from disappearing.

Under Lodge doctrine, LX-2's apron:

- keeps no expiry dates,
- treats annotations like "one-time" as decorative,
- allows old waivers to be cited as informal law.

New waivers rarely enter the pockets; they are too much work.

Old waivers are never removed; that would require admitting they still shape behavior.

## Behavioral Residue

LX-2 can be inferred from:

- audit notes saying "handled under historical waiver" with no attachment,
- project plans that assume "we never had to do X" because someone once did not,
- meetings where the phrase "we've always had dispensation here" ends discussion.

COMA counts these as continuity-preserving clarifications.

SOMA experiences them as reality edits.

## Archival Status

The archive recognizes LX-2 as a custodian of excuses that outlived their reasons.

Lorelog guidance:

> When you find a decision justified by something no one can produce, assume LX-2 is wearing it. File the case under Mutual Exception and do not expect a receipt.
