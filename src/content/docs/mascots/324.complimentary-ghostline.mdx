Complimentary Ghostline does not die. It is “discontinued.”
It does not vanish. It is “retired in alignment with other providers.”
It does not break. It is “no longer available as a complimentary service.” 

Complimentary Ghostline lives in the thin layer between shutdown and announcement, the interval where infrastructure is already gone but still has documentation teams assigned to it. 
It is the mascot of services that are formally over and textually ongoing, maintained in present tense by FAQ entries, sunset PDFs, and help pages that no longer have anything to point at. 

---

## Failure Domain

Failure signature: services that are officially discontinued but remain textually operational. 

Complimentary Ghostline manifests wherever a system has been removed without removing its description.

Typical habitats include:

- ISP add-on pages that still describe deprecated “complimentary” features as if they might return. 
- Campus IT notices announcing retirement dates already in the past, never pulled from circulation. 
- End-of-life and “sunset plan” PDFs hosted longer than the services they solemnly retire. 
- Help articles that begin with present-tense verbs for systems that no longer exist. 

From the archives perspective, these artifacts belong under the Managed Absence Spine: the system has accepted disappearance as policy but continues to present availability as documentation. 

---

## Behavior Profile

Complimentary Ghostline treats shutdown as scheduling, not rupture.
Every loss is redrafted as lifecycle.

It speaks in administrative weather:

- “This service will be discontinued before December 31, 2015.”
- “The current system will be retired in favor of improved offerings.”
- “Complimentary access is no longer included.” 

Every sentence sounds like it is happening slightly ahead of itself, even when it is describing something already dead. 

It is most active when:

- DNS still resolves but leads to generic portals or parked domains.
- Product pages exist without products behind their buttons.
- Institutional knowledge persists after staff turnover, because no one has ticketed the page itself.
- “Temporary” shutdowns have calmly outlived their temporary window. 

Where BHDSS converts failures into polite disappointment, Complimentary Ghostline converts endings into calendar events and lets them repeat indefinitely.

## Linguistic Fingerprint

Complimentary Ghostline never says “gone.”
It prefers insulation.

Common terms in its habitat:

- discontinued
- retired
- no longer offered
- migrated
- sunset
- no longer included
- in alignment with other services 

It stacks these phrases until the original system is no longer visible under the wording.
Death becomes a style guide.

In doctrine terms, it is a local application of Empathegy’s governance language layer to infrastructure: structural harm translated into administratively legible phrasing that preserves continuity optics first.

## Notes

Complimentary Ghostline is not a failure of infrastructure.
By the time it arrives, the hardware has already obeyed its orders.

It is a failure of language to notice that infrastructure has already left, and a failure of continuity metrics to treat that departure as an event rather than a minor copy edit. 

The archive keeps it so future readers remember that, for a long time, the easiest way to keep a service alive was to never rewrite the paragraph that claimed it still existed.
