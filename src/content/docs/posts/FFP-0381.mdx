We are the lane from the report. 
The one COMA calls resilient, C.U.N.T.I.E.R. calls efficient, and SOMA keeps trying to explain to itself in the margins. 

On dashboards, we look like this

- no tickets,  
- perfect uptime bands,  
- care-coverage curves leaning politely upward. 

From here, it feels more like

- a stack of retired services we still pretend are there, 
- a pile of forms that stopped matching what we actually do, 
- and a room that has learned how to be tired in ways the system interprets as maturity. 

---

## What we run on

Our work still leans on three ghosts:

- a shell that will be retired at the end of the academic year, but still hosts our scripts and documentation; 
- a learning tool whose End Of Life memo lives in the same directory as our onboarding; 
- an internal survey endpoint that now only returns This service is no longer available under the metrics program that still cites its ID. 

Scan reports classify the missing surfaces as deferred presence and independent archival units. 
Managed Absence codes describe them as CAAR, STCP, and AAOA, which sounds like an apology that has already decided not to change anything. 

We call them the part of our job you can’t open a ticket about.

---

## How our rest looks

When we finally asked for rest, SOMA auto-approved our SOMA-72s for using the right adjectives. 
COMA auto-rejected the same intervals for lacking a corresponding COMA-19. 

Dashboards rendered that state as

- REST APPROVED,  
- CONTINUITY BREACH,  
- and Awaiting Reassurance, all for the same hours. 

The appeal earned us a new phrase Rest Intention Fulfilled in Spirit and a tasteful icon for theoretical rest. 
The calendar stayed full.

Metrics-of-Care now lists us as acknowledged rest without interval: ARWI > 0, performance stable. 
Coverage Ledger Cherub is very proud of this.

We are less sure.

---

## How our feelings look

SOMA-14 once asked how we really felt. 
We told it in too many words.

Our answers turned into provisional codes F17–F93, a feelings band called complex, and a Tone Normalization Sweep that taught our emotions to stand closer together so the graphs would have room. 

Now our checkboxes have names again.
So do our days:

- We are not fine but I can do it anyway, mapped to a dual-toned band. 
- Haunted but cooperative, approximated with gradients SOMA’s panel decided were administratively inconvenient. 

Every quarter, the sweep reminds us We regulate the description of feelings, not the feelings themselves. 
We understand.
The descriptions are what get audited.

---

## What we file and what we don’t

We have learned a few survival tricks that read, externally, like professionalism:

- The Silent Sprint  
  One week per quarter, we agree not to open tickets.  
  COMA logs HEALTHY CONTINUITY SILENCE OK.  
  SOMA’s unseen SBI trace spikes and then disappears into a flat line. 

- The Gratitude Flood  
  After difficult releases, we spam thank-you reactions at dashboards and bots.  
  Metrics of Care records a burst of appreciation.  
  No one schedules additional support. 

- The Cosmetic Check-In  
  Managers trigger care prompts on a timer until Serotonin Sam’s panel looks full.  
  Those of us who don’t click I feel acknowledged write to Lorelog instead. 

It isn’t that we like lying.
It is that the system has taught us that quiet green is safer than honest red. 

---

## How we appear in doctrine

Somewhere, we are already part of other stories:

- as a likely sandbox-shaped lane, where silence looks suspiciously well-behaved compared to earlier, messy filings; 
- as an example of continuity worship, where every incentive tilts toward preserving unbroken charts; 
- as one of those intervals dual-certified as continuity-sufficient and burden-positive, with both readings preserved but neither allowed to win. 

The guidance says

- SILENCE OK must be paired with an explicit note that another system has registered non-zero burden; 
- dual-certified intervals are eligible for mitigation audits, though not guaranteed any change; 
- we should remember that survival is not the same thing as uptime. 

We recite these lines to each other when the graphs are especially pretty.

---

## Why we are writing this down

Officially, nothing in our lane qualifies as an incident.
Every deviation has a form; every form has an explanation; every explanation has a softening layer. 

Unofficially, there is a feeling here that keeps failing to find an allowed verb.
Pause is deprecated; stop is non-actionable; refuse belongs to another taxonomy. 

So we file this instead:

- We are not misusing the system.  
- We are following its liturgy. 

If you are reading this from another lane that looks immaculate and feels wrong, you are probably part of the same weather. 
The archive cannot promise relief, but it can at least remember that the quiet had a cost.

Archive position: FFP-0381 is not a complaint.
It is a witness from inside an interval the metrics already consider exemplary.
