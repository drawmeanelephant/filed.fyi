## What This Index Is For

This memorandum records recurring difficulties arising when parade maps, recovered banners, marshal sashes, and inherited route habits begin behaving like law without first enduring the humiliation of adoption.

It is intended for committees that remain unsure whether they are planning a procession or obeying one.

## Handling principles

1. Traditional route means previously favored, not inherently binding.  
Older maps may carry dignity, memory, and superior photographs. They remain answerable to barricades, emergency access, construction, weather, and the present tense.

2. Banner language may influence conduct without achieving clean authority.  
Recovered slogans, stitched maxims, and wall-mounted directives should be logged as heritage-active where the room has begun obeying them, but they should not settle labor assignments, funding disputes, or route decisions without formal adoption.

3. Regalia custody does not automatically confer office.  
A sash stored capably for many years may indicate reliability, reverence, and good cedar-closet practice. It does not by itself establish hereditary volunteerism.

These are not merely matters of tradition. They are provenance problems shaped by repeated ceremonial use. Where custody, display, and inherited handling begin carrying more authority than original adoption records, see `FREF-0570-APCR` Artifact Provenance Under Repeated Ceremonial Use.

4. Route dignity is not a traffic instrument.  
Any appeal to symbolism, visible service, historic turn sequence, or proper order of approach must survive contact with permit language and the practical existence of vehicles larger than memory.

## Restricted vocabulary

Use the following terms with restraint.

- **Traditional** — means older than this argument, not older than law.
- **Proper** — often signals preference wearing a tie.
- **The marshal's family** — not a recognized office.
- **Blessed route** — decorative unless supported by actual municipal paperwork.
- **Always** — rarely survives photo review.

## Custody note

Where regalia has remained in one household long enough to acquire moral gravity, records should distinguish storage continuity from command continuity.

Where a volunteer is described as *where the sash knows to go*, the nomination sheet should be consulted before anyone starts speaking in dynastic tones.

Where a banner phrase begins resolving disagreements faster than bylaws, doctrine is already trying to happen in the decorations.

## Filing note

Committees are reminded that a parade may remain dignified after becoming slightly practical.

This is difficult for some rooms.

The archive recommends they survive the feeling.

## Temporary corrective language

- Banner dignity is not a traffic instrument.
- Prior storage does not imply future command.
- Heritage display does not equal standing resolution.
- Any route justified only by how disappointing the photographs would otherwise look should be marked sentimental and reviewed again.
