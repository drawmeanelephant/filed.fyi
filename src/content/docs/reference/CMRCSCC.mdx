## What This Index Is For

This circular applies to commemorative ribbons, oversized scissors, dedication plaques, podium gavels, ceremonial shovels, and similar civic objects that acquire more than one locally sincere origin story.

It does not attempt to restore singular certainty where symbolic usefulness has already replaced it.

## Handling principles

1. Artifact continuity is not artifact identity.  
An object may be the ribbon everyone remembers without being the ribbon only one archive may claim cleanly.

2. Multiple origin claims are not, by themselves, evidence of bad faith.  
Where albums, minutes, captions, and mayoral photographs all describe the same object as original, the first duty is to preserve the disagreement without forcing somebody's grandmother to become wrong in public.

3. Shared custody language is preferable to heroic singularity.  
Where direct provenance has dissolved into overlapping ceremony, records should prefer terms such as *commemoratively continuous*, *jointly preserved*, *display rights rotating*, or *original use disputed*.

4. Fabric should not be asked to carry more certainty than the town can supply.  
Fray pattern comparison, satin weave notes, plaque language, and photo alignment may assist review. They do not always outrank memory rehearsed annually in good faith.

## Restricted vocabulary

Use the following terms carefully.

- **Original** — avoid unless one chain of custody survived without lunch, lamination, or municipal optimism.
- **Authentic** — use only when someone can explain what the less authentic ribbon is doing in the same display case.
- **Owned by** — avoid where three cabinets, two binders, and one sash photograph disagree.
- **Recovered from** — helpful; does not settle destiny.
- **The good ribbon** — inadequate as archival proof, though common.

## Display guidance

Where competing claims remain locally dear, the object may travel under joint seal.

Accompanying cards should identify the dispute without sounding thrilled by it.

A ribbon may be displayed as shared civic certainty even where singular origin has become administratively impolite to enforce.

## Filing note

Ribbonward Cordialis continues to observe that forcing one winner often damages more public memory than it restores.

Accordingly, some artifacts should be preserved not as settled matter, but as evidence that the town once cared in several directions at once.

This condition is not ideal.

It is, however, highly fileable.
