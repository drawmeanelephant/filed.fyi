# Support Coverage

## Purpose

This document defines Support Coverage: the degree to which a system can demonstrate that some form of supportive contact, acknowledgment, or care-coded interaction was made available, recorded, or completed across a population or interval.

Support Coverage is not relief.
It is not mitigation.
It is not proof that the support altered conditions.

It answers a narrower question:
Was a support-shaped event present in a way the system could successfully count?

---

## Foundational Rule

Support Coverage exists where:

1. supportive interactions are scheduled, prompted, delivered, or logged,
2. those interactions are countable as completed contact,
3. coverage can be expressed across intervals, cohorts, or reporting lanes,
4. the resulting measure is liable to be mistaken for the quality, depth, or effectiveness of support itself.

Coverage measures availability and recordability.
It does not certify consequence.

---

## Core Premise

Institutions often learn to count contact before they can sustain care.

Thus coverage rises quickly when systems optimize for:
- check-ins,
- acknowledgment clicks,
- completed listening prompts,
- scheduled touchpoints,
- support nudges,
- care rituals that terminate cleanly in data.

These events matter.
They may reduce isolation, soften denial, or provide a minimal witness.

But once coverage is graphed, the graph begins attracting institutional belief.
Support becomes what survives as coverage.

---

## Coverage Classes

### 1. Contact Coverage

Measures whether some support interaction occurred at all.

Examples:
- at least one check-in this week,
- one supportive message delivered,
- one reflection request completed.

---

### 2. Acknowledgment Coverage

Measures whether the recipient confirmed having been seen, heard, or acknowledged in a countable way.

Examples:
- “I feel acknowledged” click,
- validated marker,
- supportive receipt confirmation.

---

### 3. Ritual Coverage

Measures whether an institutionally sanctioned support ritual was completed, independent of downstream effect.

Examples:
- listening-board attendance,
- care huddle completion,
- manager check-in filed,
- reflection script delivered.

---

### 4. Persistence Coverage

Measures whether support recurred across time rather than occurring once.

Examples:
- weekly continuity of contact,
- multi-interval follow-up,
- repeated outreach windows.

---

### 5. Material Coverage

A narrower class in which support contact is tied to some change in schedule, load, discretion, or accommodation pathway.

Examples:
- workload altered,
- break approved,
- staffing adjusted,
- escalation granted.

Material Coverage is the most often claimed and least often evidenced.

---

## Coverage Hierarchy

Support Coverage should be read from weakest to strongest:

1. contact occurred,
2. acknowledgment was recorded,
3. ritual was completed,
4. follow-up persisted,
5. conditions changed.

Most systems can measure the first three well.
Many speak as if they had measured the fifth.

This is a routine category failure.

---

## Distinguishing Rules

Support Coverage must be distinguished from:

### Relief
The person or system became less burdened.

### Support Quality
The interaction was skilled, useful, or trusted.

### Accommodation
Practical change occurred.

### Support Availability
Help existed in theory whether or not anyone reached it.

### Emotional Satisfaction
The interaction felt good in the moment.

Coverage measures demonstrable support contact, not its adequacy or effect.

---

## Typical Inflation Sources

Support Coverage becomes inflated when:
- one-click acknowledgments close the event,
- completion is easier to record than follow-up,
- dashboards reward regular contact rather than changed conditions,
- rituals count the same whether or not they alter anything,
- scheduling systems prefer broad shallow reach over deep difficult support,
- ambiguity is normalized away to preserve comparability.

Coverage rises because systems have improved at producing countable contact, not necessarily at helping.

---

## Coverage Failure Modes

### Substitution Failure
Coverage is cited as proof of care despite unresolved strain.

### Ritual Saturation
People are repeatedly contacted without receiving leverage.

### Confirmation Collapse
A single acknowledgment marker is treated as sufficient evidence of successful support.

### Coverage Pride
High coverage becomes a reputational asset the institution is reluctant to interrogate.

### Reach Bias
Wide shallow support outranks narrow deep intervention.

### Completion Sedation
Once the support event is closed, interest in its consequences declines.

---

## Review Questions

Ask:
1. What exactly had to happen for coverage to rise?
2. Does the metric require only contact, or also follow-through?
3. What fraction of covered events changed anything material?
4. Which support forms are easiest to count and therefore most likely to dominate?
5. Who benefits from high coverage being interpreted generously?

If Question 3 cannot be answered, coverage should not be used as evidence of relief.

---

## Coverage Integrity Bands

### Contact-Only
Minimal evidence: support-shaped touch occurred.

### Acknowledged
Recipient confirmed receipt in a countable way.

### Recurrent
Support continued across intervals.

### Action-Linked
At least some covered events triggered practical change.

### Outcome-Supported
Independent evidence suggests relief or mitigation followed.

Most institutional reporting should stop before claiming the last band unless proof exists.

---

## Handling Guidance

When reporting Support Coverage:

1. specify what counts as covered,
2. separate contact from acknowledgment, acknowledgment from follow-up, and follow-up from accommodation,
3. refuse shorthand that collapses coverage into care quality,
4. note which support modes are excluded because they resist instrumentation,
5. attach deletion risk where rich interactions degrade faster than simple confirmations.

Minimum note:
“Coverage indicates recorded support contact, not verified relief.”

Stronger note:
“High coverage may reflect instrumented acknowledgment more than durable support.”

---

## Interlocks

### With Metrics of Care
Support Coverage is often the most dashboard-ready form of care substitution.

### With Visibility Exclusion
Complex support is often excluded while simple covered events dominate.

### With Acknowledgment Deletion Bias
Coverage inherits the signals that survive deletion best.

### With Rehearsed Kindness
Polished support language can improve coverage metrics without altering leverage.

### With Continuity Theatre
Coverage provides a humane-looking surface for unresolved burden.

---

## Approved Language

Preferred phrases:
- support coverage achieved
- coverage reflects counted contact
- acknowledgment present; relief unverified
- support touchpoints recorded
- covered interval, outcome unknown
- material follow-through not established

Disallowed phrases:
- coverage proves care worked
- everyone was supported
- acknowledgment equals accommodation
- full coverage means no unmet need
- repeated check-ins confirm adequate relief

---

## Archive Position

Support Coverage is what the institution knows how to count when care is too expensive, too contextual, or too unresolved to certify directly.

Empathegy permits the measure and forbids the shortcut.
