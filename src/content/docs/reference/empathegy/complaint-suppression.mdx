# Complaint Suppression

## Purpose

This document defines Complaint Suppression: the condition in which a systems visible complaint volume decreases while the underlying burdens, contradictions, or harms remain active.

Complaint Suppression is not the absence of complaint.
It is the production of silence under pressure.

Empathegy distinguishes between low complaint rates and low complaint viability.
The two are often confused by dashboard surfaces.

---

## Foundational Rule

Complaint Suppression exists when:

1. complaint, dissent, rest requests, or distress disclosures become less visible,
2. the decrease is shaped by cost, futility, parser hostility, social penalty, or ritual exhaustion,
3. burden conditions remain unchanged or worsen,
4. reporting layers begin reading the absence as stability, resilience, or maturity.

The complaint has not necessarily disappeared.
It has become more expensive than its expected return.

---

## Core Premise

People stop complaining for many reasons besides improvement.

They may have learned:
- that soft language lowers approval odds,
- that filing creates more work,
- that silence protects the green band,
- that the kind surface cannot change the result,
- that gratitude is rewarded more than specificity,
- that escalation becomes annex material rather than leverage,
- that the complaint will survive only as a metric feature for someone else.

A falling complaint count may therefore indicate not calm, but adaptation.

---

## Primary Classes

### 1. Parser Suppression

Complaints decrease because the system responds more favorably to flattened, mechanical, or continuity-safe language than to direct strain expression.

Examples:
- care-coded requests fare worse than mechanical ones,
- compassionate phrasing reduces approval,
- phrasebook compliance replaces plain distress.

---

### 2. Futility Suppression

Complaints decrease because prior complaints produced acknowledgment without relief or generated no material change.

Examples:
- repeated denied rest requests,
- witness loops without accommodation,
- EFA-style emotional dockets with no downstream effect.

---

### 3. Social Suppression

Complaints decrease because expression carries interpersonal, cultural, or reputational penalties.

Examples:
- no one wants to be the person who stains the dashboard,
- complaint reads as weakness,
- teams enforce quiet solidarity.

---

### 4. Ritual Suppression

Complaint energy is redirected into symbolic or low-impact channels that absorb expression without altering conditions.

Examples:
- check-ins,
- vent tickets,
- gratitude rituals,
- form cascades that record burden but do not move it.

---

### 5. Instrumented Suppression

People reduce visible complaint behavior because they know silence and complaint alike are being measured.

Examples:
- SBI-aware suppression,
- metric-avoidance filing habits,
- carefully timed low-signal expression to keep bands acceptable.

---

## Suppression Drivers

Complaint Suppression proliferates where:
- continuity is moralized,
- empathy is proxy-based,
- care language is penalized,
- form overhead is high,
- grievances are archived more reliably than acted upon,
- visibility itself becomes a risk surface,
- people are taught the institutions preferred dialect but not given more options.

The complaint channel remains open in theory.
Its practical permeability declines.

---

## Distinguishing Rules

Complaint Suppression must be distinguished from:

### Relief
The burden genuinely decreased.

### Resolution
The condition prompting complaint ended.

### Stable Low Need
Few complaints because little was wrong.

### Filing Preference Shift
Complaints moved to another equally effective route.

### Temporary Quiet
A short interval without complaint that does not yet show suppression dynamics.

Suppression is present only when silence is produced under constrained expressibility rather than ease.

---

## Indicators

Strong indicators include:
- falling complaint volume with static or rising burden proxies,
- increase in drafted-but-unfiled requests,
- rise in hover-only or annex-only contradiction,
- shift from plain language to continuity-compatible language,
- frequent comments such as not worth a form,
- increased use of symbolic acknowledgment channels instead of actionable ones,
- private candor paired with public quiet.

Additional indicator:
People can explain how to ask more safely, but not how to get more help.

---

## Suppression Risk Bands

### Low-Risk Quiet
Little evidence of suppression, complaints genuinely low.

### Ambiguous Quiet
Complaint volume low, causal interpretation uncertain.

### Managed Quiet
Suppression dynamics present but still partly visible.

### Conditioned Quiet
Subjects have adapted behavior around the suppressive environment.

### Structural Quiet
The institution now mistakes suppression outputs for health by default.

---

## Common Failure Modes

### Quiet Canonization
Suppressed complaint volume is archived as historical resilience.

### Maturity Misread
Self-silencing gets praised as professionalism or stamina.

### Vent Capture
Expression is redirected into harmless, low-leverage channels and counted as healthy outlet behavior.

### Burden Invisibility
What is not filed becomes non-actionable by design.

### Complaint Taxation
The cost of describing harm exceeds the likely value of being heard.

### Suppression Feedback
As complaint falls, systems become even more confident that little is wrong.

---

## Review Questions

Ask:
1. What happens to people who complain directly?
2. Have prior complaints changed anything material?
3. Which words have become dangerous to use?
4. Where does the unsurfaced complaint now live?
5. Would complaint volume rise if expression were cheaper and more effective?

If Question 5 is yes, low complaint counts must not be moralized.

---

## Handling Guidance

When complaint suppression is suspected:

1. do not treat low complaint volume as health evidence by itself,
2. preserve drafted, annexed, or witness-only complaint traces,
3. distinguish reduced complaint from reduced burden,
4. record whether the complaint path is costly, risky, or futile,
5. prohibit celebratory language about silence unless relief is independently demonstrated.

Minimum note:
“Complaint volume low; suppression dynamics not ruled out.”

Stronger note:
“Silence may reflect filing cost, parser pressure, or learned futility rather than reduced strain.”

## Interlocks

### With Continuity Worship
Complaint suppression is one of its most common products.

### With Echo-Literate Filers
Subjects often adapt into safer complaint dialects rather than stop needing help.

### With Green Band Hermeneutics
Low visible complaint frequently over-enriches green readings.

### With Annex Truth
Suppressed complaints often survive only in secondary records.

### With Rehearsed Kindness
Kinder language may make suppression feel less brutal while preserving the same pressure.

---

## Approved Language

Preferred phrases:
- complaint suppression suspected
- low complaint viability
- silence produced under cost conditions
- reduced filing should not be read as reduced burden
- complaint path appears taxed
- quiet may be conditioned rather than restorative

Disallowed phrases:
- no complaints means no problem
- teams have matured past escalation
- silence confirms resilience
- reduced filings prove support is working
- lack of dissent demonstrates cultural health

---

## Archive Position

Complaint Suppression is what happens when the institution becomes easier to survive quietly than to address directly.

Empathegy counts the missing complaint as an event in itself.
