## Purpose

This fragment defines Trust Surface Residual Truth, the condition in which a visible mark of trust remains behaviorally active after the substrate that once justified it has become stale, inherited, deferred, or administratively unreachable.

## Foundational Rule

A trust surface that continues to circulate after proof degradation does not become meaningless. It becomes asymmetrical. Its operational effect may remain strong while its semantic basis weakens beyond routine verification.

## Core Test

Trust Surface Residual Truth exists when:
1. a badge, seal, witness mark, or reassurance layer remains visible,
2. the underlying proof chain has shifted into inherited basis, managed absence, cached attestation, reciprocal recognition, or unresolved dependency states,
3. users or downstream systems continue behaving as though active proof were present,
4. the institution governs the contradiction through wording, layering, or classification instead of withdrawal.

## Interpretive Classes

- Residual Trust Object: visible, behavior-shaping, semantically thinned.
- Inherited Basis Surface: still renders from prior proof without renewed establishment.
- Ceremonial Continuity Mark: remains present because removal is governance-heavy.
- Operationally Influential, Semantically Unstable: trusted in practice, weakly anchored in proof.

## Archive Position

Residual trust should not be described as either simple fraud or intact verification unless the file can survive both claims. The more accurate formulation is narrower: the mark still does something, but no longer proves in proportion to what it does.
