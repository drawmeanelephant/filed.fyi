## Purpose

This document defines **Artifact Provenance Under Repeated Ceremonial Use**, the condition in which an object cannot be interpreted adequately by origin, authorship, or original issuance alone because repeated ritual handling has become part of what the object is.

Some artifacts begin as documents, forms, ribbons, seals, ledgers, cards, checklists, or talismans.
Over time they acquire authority through recurrence:
displayed again,
stamped again,
cited again,
carried again,
reused in new contexts,
treated as precedent because they survived prior use.

This doctrine names that accretive authority and the distortions that come with it.

## Foundational Rule

Artifact Provenance Under Repeated Ceremonial Use is active when:

1. an object has a traceable origin,
2. repeated procedural or symbolic handling materially alters its authority, meaning, or classification,
3. current use depends as much on accumulated ritual history as on original purpose,
4. institutional readers continue citing origin alone as though repeated use had not rewritten the object.

The artifact was not merely preserved. It was trained by ceremony.

## Core Premise

Archives often claim that provenance begins with who made a thing, when, and for what purpose.

That is insufficient here.

A reused form can drift into precedent.
A retired ribbon can continue to govern photographs.
A seal can become more authoritative through repetition than through scrutiny.
A talisman can inherit custody power because enough clerks agreed to handle it carefully.
A deprecated object can remain classification-active long after its initial function disappears.

In such cases, provenance is layered:
origin,
custody,
reuse,
citation,
display,
ritual burden,
and inherited interpretation.

## Primary Classes

### 1. Form Provenance Drift

A form survives beyond its designed context and accumulates authority through repeated invocation.

Examples include:
- deprecated or mismatched forms that remain mandatory attachments,
- forms reused as cautionary precedent, theatrical prop, or compliance relic,
- procedural documents whose later appearances matter more than their first approval state.

### 2. Seal and Recognition Accretion

An object appears more legitimate because recognitions, validations, or seals have accumulated around it, even where underlying review has not deepened.

Examples include:
- stacked recognitions that point back to the same validating bodies,
- over-stamped records whose original language is increasingly obscured,
- confidence surfaces generated by decorative procedural repetition.

- accreditation loop references in the Lorelog spine

### 3. Heritage Persistence

An artifact remains active in continuity or dignity surfaces after substantive authority has thinned, shifted, or ended.

Examples include:
- heritage regalia still counted in continuity displays,
- memorial or honorary presences functioning as governance-adjacent indicators,
- retired objects preserved as symbolic stewardship rather than operational authority.

Cross-reference:
- `FREF-0260-BMDH`

### 4. Custodial Sacralization

An object acquires interpretive weight because its handling is ritualized, restricted, or slowed by designated keepers.

Examples include:
- Bin 8C reindex slips, talismans, and containment tokens,
- memorial drawers and rollback tokens,
- objects whose touch permissions imply authority,
- artifacts preserved through ceremony because no cleaner legitimacy remains available.

## Provenance Layers

When evaluating an artifact, track at minimum:

1. **Origin** — who or what first produced it.
2. **Declared Purpose** — what it was said to do.
3. **Custody Path** — which desks, clerks, tribunals, mascots, or drawers kept it.
4. **Ceremonial Reuse** — where repeated handling changed its function.
5. **Display Life** — how it appeared in dashboards, photos, trainings, rituals, or executive summaries.
6. **Interpretive Burden** — what readers now expect it to mean.
7. **Residual Authority** — what it can still cause, justify, or block despite doctrinal drift.

If the final three layers outweigh the first two, provenance has become ceremonial.

## Structural Signs

This doctrine is likely active when:
- an object is cited constantly but defined weakly,
- original function is less stable than current symbolic use,
- repeated stamping, carrying, or display substitutes for fresh review,
- staff handle an artifact carefully without agreeing what it proves,
- retirement has occurred operationally but not ceremonially,
- the artifact keeps reappearing whenever legitimacy needs texture.

## Distinguishing Rules

Artifact Provenance Under Repeated Ceremonial Use must be distinguished from:

### Simple Historical Provenance
Origin remains sufficient to interpret the object.

### Heritage Display
The object is preserved decoratively without active procedural consequences.

### Annex Truth
Secondary records preserve contradiction about an event.
This doctrine concerns the object itself becoming re-authored through repeated use.

### Managed Absence
The object survives through absence classes.
Ceremonial provenance may overlap with Managed Absence when a retired object remains symbolically active, but the emphasis here is on ritual reuse as the source of continuing authority.

## Review Questions

Ask:

1. What was this object originally for?
2. How many later uses changed what it now means?
3. Which desks or rituals conferred authority through handling rather than revalidation?
4. Would the object still matter if stripped back to origin alone?
5. Is the archive preserving the artifact, or preserving its repeated use as a legitimacy machine?

If Question 4 produces a weaker object than current practice assumes, ceremonial provenance is active.

## Handling Guidance

When this condition is identified:

1. describe both original issuance and later ceremonial accretion,
2. distinguish operational authority from symbolic authority,
3. note whether repeated use clarified, distorted, or replaced provenance,
4. record custody and ritual context as part of the object, not as incidental background,
5. avoid treating survival, stamping, or display as self-authenticating legitimacy.

Minimum note:
Artifact authority reflects repeated ceremonial reuse in addition to origin.

Stronger note:
Current interpretive force derives substantially from inherited ritual handling. Origin alone no longer explains the artifact the archive is actually using.

## Approved Language

Preferred phrases:
- ceremonial provenance active
- authority accreted through reuse
- origin insufficient without custody history
- ritual handling altered interpretive status
- symbolically active beyond initial function
- repeated use became part of the artifact

Disallowed phrases:
- still official because we still use it
- provenance unchanged
- only symbolic
- just tradition
- legitimacy inherited automatically

## Archive Position

In this archive, some objects are not made once. They are made again each time they are carried forward under seal, under glass, under citation, or under the watch of someone who no longer remembers the first reason they mattered.
