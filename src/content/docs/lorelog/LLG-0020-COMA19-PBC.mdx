Form COMA-19 exists to legitimize short, necessary periods of idleness without breaking the appearance of uninterrupted continuity.[file:11]

Following the Idle-Time Amnesty Overuse incident (LLG-0019-COMA), in which teams pre-filed COMA-19 across entire weeks "for safety," the Uptime Ethics Committee requested a tighter handle on what counted as valid justification.[file:11]

The Committee concluded that "most of the overuse was semantic" and commissioned a **Phrasebook Clarification (PBC)** to align rest language with COMA's continuity objectives.

---

## Stated Purpose

PBC announces itself as:

> "A non-restrictive guide to describing necessary idle time in continuity-compatible terms."

It claims not to change eligibility for COMA-19, only to "reduce ambiguity in explanatory fields."

The document stresses that:

- COMA recognizes the importance of localized idleness.  
- COMA must also protect stakeholders from "emotive terminology" that could be misinterpreted as system failure.  
- Therefore, request language should "honor continuity while acknowledging transient throughput modulation."

SOMA representatives requested simpler wording. The Committee declined, citing the need to avoid "overly vivid" descriptions of rest.

---

## Approved Phrasing Categories

PBC defines three "continuity-compatible" justification styles for COMA-19:

1. **Throughput Realignment**  
   - Examples: "Load redistribution," "pipeline smoothing interval."  
   - Interpretation: Activity is not stopping; it is "reorganizing at a non-observable layer."  
   - Recommended for: maintenance windows, batch backlogs.

2. **Surface Quietude**  
   - Examples: "Temporarily reduced surface activity," "interface silence period."  
   - Interpretation: Only visible outputs are pausing; internal readiness remains at 100%.  
   - Recommended for: UI freezes, planned visible outages with backend continuity.

3. **Confidence Interval Maintenance**  
   - Examples: "Continuity confidence calibration," "uptime assurance rehearsal."  
   - Interpretation: The system is resting in order to prove it will not rest unannounced later.  
   - Recommended for: anything that looks too much like rest to call rest.

Phrases such as "we are tired," "we need a break," or "everyone is burning out" are discouraged on the grounds that "COMA cannot parse fatigue as a scheduling parameter."

Filers are advised to "translate subjective states into objective continuity investments."

---

## Disallowed and Translated Phrases

The Clarification includes a non-exhaustive table of phrases that "may lead to misinterpretation" and their approved substitutions:

- "We are exhausted" → "Current throughput exceeds sustainable equilibrium."  
- "We cannot think straight" → "Decision quality metrics trend below continuity baseline."  
- "Everyone is crying" → "Output stability indicators exhibit moisture-adjacent variance."

Requests containing unapproved phrases are not rejected; they are "normalized" by the auto-issuance daemon before entering the ledger.[file:11]

Several internal parser revisions continued classifying repeated "confidence maintenance intervals" as latent rest requests despite successful phrase normalization.

SOMA objected that this practice erases the emotional content of rest requests. COMA replied that emotional content is "out of scope for continuity modeling."

---

## Interaction with SOMA-72

Because SOMA-72 rest requests often reference feelings directly, PBC instructs COMA staff to:

- Treat SOMA-72 language as context, not as COMA-19 justification.  
- Prefer internal COMA-19 phrasing even when SOMA-72 describes the situation more accurately.  
- Record any mismatch as "cross-directive interpretive divergence," not as an error.[file:11]

In practice:

- SOMA-72 might say: "The team is not coping."  
- COMA-19, after phrase normalization, records: "Current load distribution invites continuity recalibration."

This divergence is logged as evidence of robust governance, rather than misalignment.

LLG-0072-SOMA notes that many periods are now "emotionally acknowledged, operationally denied" with phrasebook-compliant language attached.[file:11]

---

## Uptime Optics and Dashboard Effects

Post-PBC, dashboards exhibit:

- Fewer explicit mentions of rest; more references to "confidence maintenance."  
- A decline in "amnesty" labels and a rise in "scheduled continuity rehearsals."  
- No measurable reduction in actual downtime events.

COMA interprets this as "successful alignment of language with desired narrative."

SOMA reports increased confusion among staff, who state privately that "we have never worked harder to sound like we are not pausing."

The Tri-Directive Reconciliation Council cites PBC alongside LLG-0331-TPI as an example of "systems that preserve documentation of harm while removing its self-description."[file:11]

---

## Staff Feedback (Internal Use Only)

The Ethics Committee requested anonymous comments from amnesty filers to evaluate the Phrasebook's impact.

Common themes include:

- "I spend more time translating how bad it is than actually stopping doing the thing that is bad."  
- "We have learned to schedule our collapse as a confidence drill."  
- "I no longer know whether we are resting or just hiding rest under continuity vocabulary."

One comment, preserved in full in this record and nowhere else, reads:

> "COMA-19 used to be a way to admit we couldn't keep going like this for a while.  
>  Now it's a way to promise we can absolutely keep going like this, provided we call it something else."


The Committee's response note: *"Perception of burden increased. Continuity narratives improved. Trade-off acceptable."*

Cross-system audit note appended post-closure indicates that phrasebook compliance not only affects COMA-19 eligibility but also correlates with downstream resource prioritization signals in unrelated allocation and empathy scoring systems. Requests encoded in continuity-compatible phrasing exhibit higher persistence across scheduling, allocation, and escalation systems regardless of underlying operational strain. This correlation is recorded as non-causal in COMA, and as structurally informative in SOMA. DXS commentary later identified this effect as a form of linguistic selection pressure operating across rest eligibility pathways.

---

## Resolution and Residuals

PBC is marked **resolved** on the grounds that:

- COMA-19 filings have returned to pre-overuse levels.  
- Dashboards no longer show large blocks labeled "amnesty"; they show "calibration," "rehearsal," and "interval confidence."  
- No new explicit rest category has been created.

However:

- SOMA-72 incidents citing "phrasebook fatigue" are on the rise.[file:11]  
- LLG-0334-CSI records at least one interval COMA certified as "healthy silence" that SOMA logged as "unprocessed strain."[file:11]

The Lorelog Appeals Queue has accepted an appeal arguing that PBC constitutes "semantic cruelty."

No tribunal has agreed to hear it yet; all have requested additional reassurance that reframing rest as "confidence maintenance" is not, in itself, a continuity risk.

A late-stage internal annotation appended during archival consolidation notes that repeated use of continuity-compatible phrasing across COMA-19 submissions correlates with a progressive reduction in the system’s ability to distinguish between operational rest events and their descriptive representations. Over successive review cycles, operational rest states are increasingly inferred through continuity-compatible linguistic indicators rather than direct observation channels.

This effect is recorded in SOMA commentary as “loss of access to unmediated strain signals,” and in COMA commentary as “successful stabilization of interpretive consistency under Phrasebook conditions.”

No remediation pathway has been proposed, as the divergence is currently classified as non-actionable variance within expected continuity modeling drift.
