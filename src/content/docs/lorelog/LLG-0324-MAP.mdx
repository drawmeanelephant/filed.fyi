In response to recurring questions about whether certain forms “still existed,” the Central Forms Catalog drafted the **Managed Absence Protocol (MAP)**.

MAP defines how the registry should speak about forms that are not straightforwardly present or gone: deprecated templates still in use, unofficial drafts, mutually superseding revisions, and paperwork that appears only in historical Lorelog entries.

Rather than correcting these states, MAP codifies them into a taxonomy of **governed absences**.

---

## Protocol Definitions

Under MAP, a form may be classified as:

- **Conceptually Active, Administratively Retired (CAAR):**  
  The form is no longer issued, but its obligations are assumed to persist via “equivalent intent” in newer paperwork.

- **Locally Canonical, Globally Unrecognized (LCGU):**  
  A form that appears only in one office’s workflows but has been in use long enough that withdrawing it would generate more incidents than leaving it in place.

- **Superseded in Theory, Co-Equal in Practice (STCP):**  
  Two or more forms that each list the others as retired while all remain valid in at least one policy document.

- **Archivally Asserted, Operationally Absent (AAOA):**  
  Forms that exist solely in Lorelog case files or training slides: present enough to cite, missing enough that nobody can locate a blank copy.

MAP instructs registry staff to **select the kind of absence** that best maintains continuity optics, then continue as if that state were intentional.

---

## Implementation Guidance

The Registry Interpretation Office circulated a memo with sample language:

- “Form 40-C is now CAAR; please use the new collaboration declaration sequence wherever its spirit applies.”  
- “Form 51-E-A is LCGU; do not remove it until a more official workaround develops folklore.”  
- “Refer to 12-A legacy variants as STCP; choose whichever one offends the fewest dashboards.”  

Auditors were advised that discovering a missing or contradictory form should trigger a MAP classification, not a defect ticket.

Bricky noted that “managed absence” was still absence, but the phrase tested better during audits.

The Protocol was promptly marked **resolved** upon publication, on the grounds that ambiguity had been successfully renamed into a governed condition.

Subsequent absence-related incidents (SRO, MEC, OIR) all cite MAP as precedent, often without agreeing which category applies.
