The Council drafted Form 88-B as a ceremonial protocol for thermal events: a structured series of
acknowledgements, shutdown chants, and fan checks meant to bring overheated systems down without
panic. It assumed late-night maintenance windows, empty offices, and no audience.

On 2024-10-18, a legacy hardware showcase stream ignored all of that.

At 19:42 local time, server cluster FURNACE-03 began to spike. Fans ramped, casing warmed, and
Boily McPlaterton manifested in the overlay with a temperature readout and a look that said
*I told you so.* When internal thresholds crossed the ceremonial limit, he did what the protocol
required: he invoked Form 88-B—out loud.

The livestream transcript records:

> “We are now initiating Emergency Cooldown Protocol Form 88-B. This is not a drill.  
> Step one: admit you should have cleaned the vents.”
>
> [CHAT] “lmao he has a form for this”  
> [CHAT] “IS THIS REAL PAPERWORK”  
> [CHAT] “FANS SOUND LIKE A HELICOPTER”

Boily proceeded to:

- Read the cooldown chant verbatim from Form 88-B while the host tried to keep presenting.
- Demand a full shutdown sequence be observed “for the feelings of the silicon.”
- File a preliminary incident report into the archive mid-stream, including viewer count.

Temperatures fell. The stream survived. The host did not.

Post-incident review concluded:

- Form 88-B is effective even when executed under ridicule.
- The protocol’s assumption that “no one will be watching” was incorrect.
- Boily’s insistence on ceremonial language (“We cool our sins, not just our chips”) is now
  considered part of the spec.

Revisions:

- Form 88-B now contains a checkbox: “Executed under live audience conditions (Y/N).”
- A new note clarifies: *If Y, you must also file a separate dignity-loss report.*
- Future thermal events involving mascots must log case number LLG-0088-B as precedent.

Resolution: RESOLVED. The hardware cooled. The archive gained a better story. Boily remains
smug and unrepentant, with breedingProgram eligibility unchanged.
