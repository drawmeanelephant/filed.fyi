## Object Summary

LC-22 Statement of Self-Esteem for Backups is cited in multiple manifest review procedures as a mandatory or recommended attachment.  
Its purpose is variously described as:

- “affirmation of backup confidence,”
- “self-assessed readiness for restore,” and
- “psychological safety overlay for snapshot review.” 

No single, agreed-upon template has been located.  
Fragments embedded in older manifests suggest that LC-22 asked backups to speak for themselves.

## Known References

LC-22 is mentioned explicitly in:

- LLG-0316-MBR Backup Rot Manifest, as “the statement we never had time to ask for.”  
- LLG-0318-SRO Scan Report Orphan Files, where orphaned snapshots are noted as lacking “even the courtesy of LC-22.”  
- Internal training materials for the Snapshot Affirmation Desk, which instruct reviewers to “ensure LC-22 is present where confidence is low and storage is cheap.” 

These references agree that LC-22 should exist, disagree about its form, and do not include a full copy.

## Probable Structure (Reconstructed)

Extracted from marginal quotations, LC-22 likely contained:

- A header tying it to a specific backup set or snapshot window.
- A first-person statement from the backup, such as “I believe I contain what you think I contain.”
- Multiple choice or scaled responses about:

  - perceived completeness,
  - comfort with being restored,
  - awareness of prior partial failures. 

Some versions appear to have included a free-text field labeled “If restored, I would most like to remember…”, which reviewers marked as non-operational and skipped.

## Directive Interpretations

**SOMA** treated LC-22 as a care ritual for infrastructure: a way to acknowledge that backups exist in a state of uncertain expectation, often touched only during crises.  
The act of asking a backup “how it feels” to be restored or not restored was seen as correcting an asymmetry in attention. 

**COMA** regarded LC-22 as non-binding narrative: any assertion not validated by checksum was logged as “expressed confidence” but did not affect continuity scoring.  
Reports listing LC-22 were accepted so long as numeric freshness metrics remained within thresholds. 

**C.U.N.T.I.E.R.** evaluated LC-22 briefly as a potential source of engagement telemetry for storage systems, then discarded it as “ungraphable sentiment without product hooks.”  
No dashboard field was ever reserved for backup self-esteem. 

## Assurance Vocabulary Overlay

In the wake of LLG-0323-ASD, some LC-22 excerpts were retroactively harmonized:

- “I am not sure” → “confidence window remains open.”
- “I feel incomplete” → “differential potential detected.”
- “I have never been restored” → “untested readiness condition.” 

These substitutions preserved the appearance of assurance while diluting the original uncertainty.

AV-14 Nullseal Register is known to have sealed several LC-22-derived phrases, making them difficult to distinguish from standard continuity language. 

## Managed Absence and Orphan Inventory

Under LLG-0329-OIR Orphan Inventory Reframing, backups lacking LC-22 are classified as:

- structurally present,
- emotionally unconsulted, and
- eligible for retention decisions without prior conversation. 

LC-22 thus functions as a threshold artifact:

- where present, it complicates deletion by invoking “self-reported readiness,”
- where absent, it offers an alibi for treating backups as purely technical. 

Many orphaned snapshots carry comments such as “LC-22 not applicable” or “statement not requested at time of capture,” suggesting that the rite was known but unevenly applied.

## Fragments and Excerpts

Recoverable lines attributed to LC-22 include:

- “If restored, I will behave as if I was always meant to be current.”  
- “I contain states you no longer recall asking for.”  
- “My checksum passes; my experience of time has not been verified.” 

In several manifests, the LC-22 section is redacted with the note:

> Narrative withheld; integrity confirmed numerically.  

No policy explains why narrative was removed if it never counted as evidence.

## Archival Classification

LC-22 is indexed as a Low-Confidence Object:

- it is heavily referenced,
- its form is contested,
- its effect on decisions is inferred but not documented. 

Lorelog guidance for catalogers reads:

> When you encounter a manifest that behaves as though it has spoken for itself, assume LC-22 was present, even if the statement is not.  

Attempts to reconstruct a canonical LC-22 template have been deferred on the grounds that any new version would erase evidence of prior ambiguity.

The Statement of Self-Esteem for Backups remains, appropriately, more believed in than observed.
