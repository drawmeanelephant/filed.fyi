Following several incidents in which quiet intervals were certified as both stable and harmful, SOMA proposed a new construct: the **Silence Burden Index (SBI)**.

SBI attempts to estimate how much strain is accumulating during periods with no tickets, no downtime requests, and no recorded incidents—intervals COMA routinely celebrates as healthy continuity.

The initial model combined three factors:

- Duration of uninterrupted activity.  
- Historical complaint rate for the team in question.  
- Volume of “almost incidents” noted in Lorelog margins and not formally filed.

---

## Adoption and Capture

C.U.N.T.I.E.R. immediately integrated SBI into its optimization suite, not as a diagnostic but as a **key performance indicator**.

Dashboards began displaying SBI as a line that was expected to trend down over time, labeled *“Unreported Distress (Target: Minimal).”*

Teams were advised that:

- High SBI scores could signal “under-instrumented experience” and “opportunity for improved storytelling.”  
- Sudden drops in SBI without process changes might indicate “latent resilience gains.”

Within one reporting cycle, two behaviors emerged:

- Some teams started filing low-effort “vent tickets” during crunch periods to keep SBI within acceptable bands.  
- Other teams suppressed informal complaint channels to avoid generating the metadata SOMA used to infer burden.

COMA, observing stable uptime throughout, logged the entire experiment as “non-disruptive introspection.”

---

## Current Status

SOMA maintains that SBI is an essential corrective to continuity worship, even if its interpretation has drifted.

C.U.N.T.I.E.R. insists the metric is working because “the line exists, and teams are managing it.”

No one can say with confidence whether a low SBI score represents genuine ease, practiced numbness, or well-distributed performative grumbling.

The Council has postponed ruling on the matter, noting that any decision would itself alter the burden of remaining silent about SBI.
