DOGE’s charter assumes that origin, agency, and residue can be tested
in sequence. Edge cases are what happens when they arrive entangled.

Three clusters recur often enough to require their own file:

1. **Grief Relays** — local losses learned at a distance,
   where origin belongs to the loss, delivery to the feed,
   and agency is limited to the choice of how to grieve.

2. **Synthetic Care** — relationships with entities under
   breedingProgram jurisdiction, where Origin is non-local
   but the care itself clearly arises in the filer.

3. **Counterfactual Anger** — situations where a real local harm
   is only discoverable via platform, and evaluators must decide
   whether anger that arrived through a feed would have existed
   without it.

---

## EC-001 — Grief Relay

In EC-001, a filer learned of a death through a social feed
before any direct contact.

- Origin: the death, which occurred in the filer's real social world.
- Delivery: a feed, optimized for engagement.
- Residue: lasting behavioral and narrative change.

DOGE initially classified the experience as feed-adjacent.
On appeal, the Tribunal held that the platform was the messenger,
not the origin.

Precedent: when a specific, verifiable event in the filer’s world
precedes the platform encounter, Origin must be assigned to that event,
even if the first awareness was mediated.

---

## EC-002 — Synthetic Care (breedingProgram adjacency)

Several cases involve filers describing sustained care for
synthetic-origin entities: mascots, tools, and archive processes
they refuse to treat as disposable.

Under a strict Origin reading, these experiences would never
qualify as Genuine, because the object of care arrived as software.

DOGE’s adopted position, recorded here for reference:

> A genuine experience of care does not require the object of care
> to have originated locally. It requires the care itself to have.

Origin is therefore evaluated on the *filer’s* side in such cases.
If the care arises in their actual life and leaves residue,
DOGE may certify it despite synthetic objects.

## EC-003 — Counterfactual Anger (see LLG-0363-RAGE-MISIDENTIFICATION)

In RAGE misidentification appeals, filers argue that their anger
belongs to a local harm, not to the way a feed packaged it.

The Tribunal’s practical rule:

- When there is a concrete local event, and
- the filer can articulate a clear tie to that event,

DOGE may treat anger as locally anchored, even if it arrived via feed.

The unresolved question is whether evaluators should try to decide
what the filer *would* have felt in a world where the platform
had not told them.

Kindy files this question under provenance, pending a better word.
