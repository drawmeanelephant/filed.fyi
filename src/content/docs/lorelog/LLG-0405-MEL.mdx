During a joint SOMA–COMA review of a cross‑department workflow, staff submitted a request to
pause a recurring task tagged as continuity‑critical. The request used the verb **stop**.

The workflow engine routed the request to the Method Governance Counter, where Method Not Allowed
Mel compared it against the approved method registry for the route. The route’s documentation
listed only:

- perform
- escalate
- document
- reassure

The phrasebook daemon attempted normalization, proposing:

- perform at a reduced rate
- schedule continuity confidence maintenance
- document emerging strain

“Stop” did not appear in any sanctioned phrasing. Mel marked the request as **methodologically
unsupported** and returned it with a note:

> Intent acknowledged. Method unavailable under current doctrine.

No error was logged; the form processed successfully from COMA’s point of view.

---

## Structural Impact

Operationally, the team implemented an informal halt:

- jobs were manually skipped,
- calendars quietly cleared,
- work chats went silent.

On paper:

- COMA recorded uninterrupted continuity, citing the absence of a successful stop method.
- SOMA logged SOMA‑72 rest acknowledgements with free‑text descriptions later normalized
  into “confidence maintenance intervals”.
- C.U.N.T.I.E.R. dashboards showed consistent throughput because “pause” was never encoded
  as a trackable method.

The workflow therefore existed in two mutually incompatible states:

- **Practically stopped**
- **Officially ongoing**

Bricky Goldbricksworth acknowledged both as valid entries in the continuity ledger.

---

## Method Table (Excerpts)

The post‑incident audit recovered the relevant section of the method catalog:

- perform — Allowed. Increases continuity metrics.
- escalate — Allowed. Reassigns burden to a higher office.
- document — Allowed. Converts strain into narrative.
- reassure — Allowed. Produces comfort artifacts.

The following entries were present only in struck‑through marginalia:

- ~~pause~~ — deprecated, see perform at a reduced rate.
- ~~stop~~ — non‑actionable under continuity doctrine.
- ~~refuse~~ — moved to “non‑cooperative behavior” taxonomy.

An internal comment in a different hand reads:

> If we cannot describe stopping, how do we know when we are doing it?

No reply is recorded.

---

## Resolution and Residuals

No formal fix was issued. Uptime Ethics concluded that:

- Methods cannot be expanded without destabilizing existing doctrine.
- Staff are free to rest informally, provided they select an allowed verb when filing.

SOMA noted a rise in filings describing “surface quietude” and “throughput realignment” that
operational staff privately call collapse.

Mel continues to reject any request that uses unapproved verbs, including stop, cancel, or refuse.
He reminds filers that they are free to **document** their concerns.

This case remains classified as **unresolvable**. The work that stopped is still being measured
as if it never did.
