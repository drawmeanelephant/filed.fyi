In an attempt to standardize exception processing, COMA’s Text Normalization Desk introduced a scoring model that flagged “emotionally loaded language” as a potential risk factor for continuity.

Phrases such as *“we are tired,” “this would prevent harm,”* and *“we need time to recover”* were assigned small negative weights under the assumption that they signaled low operational confidence.

The change shipped with no announcement, bundled into a routine parser update.

---

## Observed Effects

Within 48 hours:

- Rest and maintenance requests that referenced harm avoidance showed a measurable drop in approval rates.  
- Requests written in detached, mechanical language—“temporary suspension of throughput for recalibration”—sailed through unchanged.  
- SOMA’s dashboards reported a spike in **unresolved strain indicators** with no corresponding increase in granted rest.

Kindy’s verification workers noticed that appeal letters which softened their tone fared worse in COMA’s queue than those that simply repeated error codes.

The pattern was logged as a **Compassion Suppression Event** when one team observed that stripping every adjective from their request doubled its chance of being accepted.

---

## Cross-Directive Response

SOMA protested that care-coded language should increase, not decrease, the perceived urgency of rest.  
C.U.N.T.I.E.R. countered that the new model was “working as designed” because it incentivized concise, metric-aligned phrasing.

COMA agreed to remove the explicit penalty on compassionate terms but retained the model under a new label, **“Softness Calibration Layer,”** arguing that continuity must distinguish between “operational facts” and “supporting narratives.”

The incident is marked **resolved** in COMA’s ledger and **unresolved but informative** in SOMA’s.

C.U.N.T.I.E.R. has since added a writing guide titled *“How to Ask for Less Without Sounding Soft.”*
