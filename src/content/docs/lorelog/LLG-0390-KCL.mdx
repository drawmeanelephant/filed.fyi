The kitchen books at Fellowship Hall were designed to solve a simple problem: who brought what, who should be thanked, and whether the serving trays returned to the correct cupboards. Over time the books became better at preserving esteem than tracking labor.

A review triggered by duplicated reimbursement envelopes found that several dish cards still defaulted to names no longer cooking, no longer attending, or no longer alive. The defaults were not random. They clustered around beloved volunteers whose recipes had become local liturgy.

This produced a persistence loop:
- The card carried the elder's name because everyone associated the dish with her.
- The room thanked the elder because the card carried the name.
- The ledger credited the elder because the room thanked her.
- New cooks entered only in margins, initials, or not at all.

The result was not fraud. It was grief expressed as metadata.

Sister Casserole of Relief classified the condition as Auxiliary Labor Afterlife, defined here as the continued accrual of civic gratitude by an identity whose material contribution has migrated elsewhere. Her audit distinguished three statuses:
- prepared by
- prepared from the card of
- prepared in honor of

Only the first counts as active labor. The hall had been using all three interchangeably because they felt kinder that way.

Financial exposure remained minor, though one annual thank-you plaque had effectively become posthumous payroll in decorative form. The larger issue was custodial: living volunteers reported feeling warmly invisible, while older names grew steadily more operational with each supper.

Interim corrections:
- Dish cards must separate recipe lineage from current labor.
- Thank-you remarks may remain ceremonial.
- Credit tallies used for budgeting, plaques, or election eligibility must not be sourced from sentimental defaults.

Resolution remains pending because the corrected cards look colder on the table, and several members have asked whether accuracy is worth making the room feel less held.
