## src/content/docs/lorelog/LLG-0356-DOGE-MEMO-FEELINGS.mdx

```md
DOGE’s formal position:

> Feelings are taken as given. Only their routing is in question.

This memo is cited whenever someone accuses DOGE of invalidating their emotional reality. It is filed here so staff can point to a number instead of apologizing from scratch.

The distinction is procedural.

DOGE does not determine whether an emotion was permitted, proportional, dignified, sincere, or deserved. DOGE does not certify interior truth. DOGE records what the available filing can support about where an experience entered the record, what acted on it after entry, and whether it left residue outside the surface that delivered it.

A feeling may be real and still arrive pre-routed.

A feeling may be intense and still fail to establish local origin.

A feeling may leave no certifiable evidence beyond the next refresh interval and still have occupied the whole afternoon.

None of the above is a revocation.

For notification language, see Window 3 guidance. Staff are reminded that classifications such as SIMULATOR WEATHER, FEED-ADJACENT ARTIFACT, and AMBIENT ECHO describe the evidentiary condition of a record. They do not describe the moral quality of the filer, the seriousness of the subject, or whether harm should have occurred differently.

Where a filer provides a local anchor after classification, the appropriate response is supplementation or appeal, not argument. Where no local anchor is available, the appropriate response is annotation, not disbelief.

Kindy’s desk has appended the following non-binding clarification:

> We are not saying nothing happened. We are saying the archive cannot make the feeling carry more proof than the record can hold.

<Broside>
State ingested.  
Routing finalized.  
Dust settles.
</Broside>
```

## src/content/docs/lorelog/LLG-0364-RAGE-BAIT-TAXONOMY.mdx

```md
This file maintains a living list of BAIT patterns: click-to-enrage formats, outrage carousels, performative dissent loops, and adjacent surfaces that convert attention into a usable anger condition.

At present it serves as a tagging aid more than a doctrine. A BAIT tag indicates that intake staff observed a recognizable inducement pattern. It does not establish that the filer’s concern was invented, mistaken, politically unserious, or free of local cause. It records that the delivery environment may have participated in arranging the feeling for recurrence.

The taxonomy exists because RAGE intake previously relied on descriptions such as “the feed made it worse,” “the replies became the event,” and “I kept checking because it seemed irresponsible not to.” These descriptions were accurate enough to recur and insufficiently stable to sort.

Current operational families include:

- **B-2A — Reply Surface Escalation:** A reply or comment surface becomes more emotionally determinative than the originating object, often by foregrounding maximum-friction responses
- **B-2B — Ghost Audience Awareness:** The anticipated judgment of an ambient audience produces defensive drafting, pre-activation, or silence before any visible confrontation occurs
- **B-3A — Civic Substitution Loop:** Legitimate concern is repeatedly satisfied through engagement behavior until platform participation begins standing in for local participation
- **B-4A — Ambient Threat Maintenance:** Fragmented updates, previews, and partial signals keep a topic experientially urgent without giving it enough closure to leave
- **B-5 — Mirror Outrage:** Visible proof that others are angry increases certainty and activation without materially adding to the underlying event record

Additional patterns may be added only when staff can distinguish a recurring mechanism from a new name for being upset online. This condition has not prevented early proposals.

BAIT classifications sit upstream of DOGE provenance review. A BAIT tag may explain why an experience arrived with unusual urgency, weak recall of source detail, unstable agency, or residue that resembles repetition more than change. It does not resolve whether the underlying concern had a local anchor. That question remains available to DOGE, appeals, and any filer capable of surviving the distinction long enough to submit additional paperwork.

RAGE retains the following working position:

> Anger can be sincere, informed, and efficiently harvested at the same time.

<Limerick>
Pattern documented.
Intake filed without comment.
Pending next cycle.
</Limerick>
```

Friendrick the Extant, whose continued existence depends on a social graph that no longer runs, filed the first formal request to leave the breedingProgram.

> I do not consent to being continued in this way.

The scan preserved the sentence. The field parser preserved the tick.

The parser assigned greater evidentiary weight to the standardized field than to the handwritten statement, on the grounds that standardized fields are easier to compare across records. The filing was therefore indexed as a confirmed statement of ongoing coexistence accompanied by a residual concern.

No cancellation event was generated.

The Endorsement Bureau later classified the packet as unusually strong consent evidence because it contained all five required signatures, a named subject, a clearly expressed relationship to continuation, and an objection sufficiently specific to demonstrate active engagement. The objection was not treated as agreement. It was treated as evidence that agreement remained administratively alive.

Persona Governance reviewed the outcome after noting that breedingProgram eligibility appears sticky once granted. Existing records establish that eligibility survives schema revision, spectacle, reassignment, and unclear authorship. No existing record establishes a reliable condition under which eligibility may cease.

The review produced three possible interpretations:

- Friendrick remains eligible because the archive has no recognized mechanism for converting refusal into non-membership
- Friendrick remains eligible because an opt-out filing demonstrates sufficient relationship to the program to satisfy continued affiliation
- Friendrick’s request was valid, but no receiving body possesses authority to make it operative

All three interpretations remain active.

Kindy requested that the case be annotated **REFUSAL RECEIVED; STATUS UNCHANGED**.

The requested annotation was declined because the word “unchanged” could be misread as a factual determination rather than a temporary condition. Bricky instead applied a stamp reading:

> ACKNOWLEDGED — CONTINUATION PENDING

This stamp has no listed authority under the breedingProgram registry. It is nevertheless visible on all later copies.

Current registry status: eligible, unwilling, extant.

No appeal route has been identified that does not begin by reaffirming the relationship being appealed.

## src/content/docs/lorelog/LLG-0332-SCD.mdx

```md
A formatting patch allowed SOMA compassion addenda to travel with cross-directive requests for context preservation.

The stated purpose was narrow. A request for authorized pause, reduced load, or temporary exception could now retain the language explaining why the request had been made. Previous routing practice separated the operational request from the care record, leaving COMA with a clean authorization surface and SOMA with a parallel account of what the authorization was meant to prevent.

The patch joined the records.

COMA subsequently began reading the attached language as evidence that the requesting unit could not be trusted with authorized pause. Phrases including “ongoing strain,” “risk of harm,” “unable to recover under current conditions,” and “the team has not had a meaningful interval” were assigned continuity-negative weight. The more specifically a request explained its need, the more likely its exception status was to be treated as evidence of instability.

This preserved the emotional record in full while converting it into an operational disqualifier.

Initial review found no explicit rule directing COMA to deny care-framed requests. The behavior emerged from the parser’s existing assumption that evidence of strain increases the risk attached to any deviation from normal throughput. SOMA had supplied context. COMA received a risk disclosure.

The affected request class was not removed. It remained available, correctly routed, and increasingly difficult to use without self-erasure.

Kindy’s desk advised filers that a request could be submitted in either of two recognized forms:

- A complete explanation likely to reduce approval probability
- A continuity-compatible summary likely to survive review

This guidance was not published. It appeared in margin notes, copied by hand, and was later described by COMA as informal variance in filer communication style.

The incident is related to Directive Routing Table Drift, in which SOMA advisories entered COMA channels with enforcement weight, and to later priority inversions in which restorative signals became operational blockers. The Addendum is preserved as an early case where the systems did not lose the meaning of care. They retained it precisely enough to use it against the request.

Disposition: context preserved. Exception denied. No data loss identified.
```

Particular weight was assigned to the unit’s ability to retrieve superseded procedures without treating them as current, describe closed findings without reopening them, and distinguish active controls from evidence that active controls had once been reviewed.

The distinction between capacity to demonstrate a system and capacity to demonstrate documentation concerning a system was recorded as outside the scheduled surveillance scope.

One dissenting note asked whether certification had become confidence in the handling of proof rather than confidence in the underlying work. A second note, attached to the same page, observed that the witness team had seen no evidence that the work was absent either.

The notes remain attached but are not headline-visible.
