```markdown
# Replacement Without Release

## Purpose

This document defines Replacement Without Release: the condition in which a system, process, or governance layer is introduced to replace a prior failure, exception, or workaround, but the inherited burden remains behaviorally necessary after the replacement arrives.

The new layer presents succession.
The old condition continues as operating basis.

The archive records this not as failed replacement, but as replacement whose obligation boundary never cleared.

---

## Core Thesis

1. Every fix inherits a tolerance for the damage that justified it.
2. The workaround enters through the side door and leaves as policy.
3. A fix becomes infrastructure when the exception starts carrying traffic.
4. Compatibility with prior breakage is not evidence of repair.
5. Contradiction distributed across layers remains contradiction.
6. The replacement arrived to remove the exception and stayed to manage it.
7. Systems age by preserving the conditions they were built to end.

---

## Foundational Rule

Replacement Without Release exists when all or most of the following are true:

1. A new structure is introduced as corrective, successor, or stabilizing layer.
2. The prior defect, exception path, or workaround is said to be retired, reduced, or superseded.
3. Operational continuity still depends on assumptions inherited from that earlier condition.
4. The inherited assumptions are no longer treated as temporary.
5. Compatibility language substitutes for proof of structural correction.
6. The new layer increasingly governs the old burden instead of ending it.

When these conditions hold, the archive treats the prior exception as inherited basis rather than residual noise.

---

## Doctrine

1. A condition may be representationally stable while remaining operationally unresolved.
2. Where a workaround remains behaviorally necessary, treat it as inherited basis, not residue.
3. Continuity language shall not be mistaken for structural resolution.
4. Managed compatibility with a defect is not correction.
5. When a workaround becomes prerequisite, the exception has entered ordinary governance.
6. Trust surfaces may remain active after direct proof conditions have entered inheritance.
7. The archive distinguishes between retired obligation and renamed persistence.

---

## Primary Classes

### 1. Inherited Basis Drift

A successor system adopts the assumptions of the failure it replaced.

Signs include:
- replacement logic preserving prior thresholds,
- new tooling depending on old exception semantics,
- retirement language paired with unchanged burden routing.

### 2. Exception Load-Bearing

A temporary accommodation becomes necessary infrastructure.

Signs include:
- fallback paths carrying routine volume,
- support structures built around the temporary state,
- removal becoming more disruptive than retention.

### 3. Residual Enforcement Drift

Workaround behavior becomes enforced baseline through repetition and dependency.

Signs include:
- reviewers expecting workaround-shaped inputs,
- informal accommodations becoming mandatory formatting,
- repeated local fixes hardening into procedural normal.

### 4. Compliance Skin

A correction surface preserves prior accommodation patterns while presenting a resolved appearance.

Signs include:
- cleaner forms for unchanged obligations,
- improved wording around structurally identical handling,
- calm visual closure over unresolved dependency.

### 5. Distributed Nonresolution

A problem remains active because its contradictions have been successfully divided across layers.

Signs include:
- one layer preserving burden,
- another preserving continuity,
- another preserving legitimacy,
- no layer carrying total contradiction plainly enough to trigger release.

### 6. Exception Provenance Loss

The original reason for a workaround disappears after the workaround becomes standard.

Signs include:
- staff can perform the ritual but cannot state the cause,
- policy cites necessity without incident memory,
- inherited handling survives after its founding evidence thins out.

---

## Distinguishing Rules

Replacement Without Release must be distinguished from:

### Repair
The underlying contradiction is materially reduced or ended.

### Managed Coexistence
Two systems knowingly remain active without claiming one has replaced the other.

### Transitional Drift
Inheritance persists briefly during migration and remains clearly temporary.

### Decorative Legacy
An old form remains visible without materially governing present behavior.

This doctrine applies only when inherited workaround logic still affects practical routing, burden, legitimacy, or decision behavior.

---

## Typical Signs

Common indicators include:
- successor systems preserving prior exception pathways as prerequisites,
- old burden reappearing under calmer names,
- announced repair alongside preserved compatibility with the defect,
- support structures created around what was meant to disappear,
- local contradiction resolved only in presentation,
- trust, routing, or policy surfaces remaining attached to inherited assumptions,
- inability to remove the old accommodation without visible operational pain.

A key sign:
the institution can explain why the replacement exists more easily than it can demonstrate what the replacement released.

---

## Structural Oppositions

The archive indexes this doctrine through five recurring oppositions:

- Repair vs Compatibility
- Resolution vs Distribution
- Retired Obligation vs Renamed Persistence
- Structural Correction vs Administrative Calm
- Symbolic Completion vs Verified Change

These oppositions should be preserved, not prematurely reconciled.

---

## Review Questions

Ask:

1. What prior condition was the replacement supposed to end?
2. Which assumptions from that condition still govern present behavior?
3. What would break if inherited accommodation were actually removed?
4. Is compatibility being cited as evidence of correction?
5. Has the workaround become ordinary enough that its origin no longer matters operationally?
6. Which layer still carries the contradiction, and which layers merely soften its appearance?

If Question 3 produces meaningful operational damage, release never occurred.

---

## Handling Guidance

When Replacement Without Release is present:

1. identify the inherited basis explicitly,
2. separate continuity from correction in all summaries,
3. prohibit compatibility claims from standing in for repair evidence,
4. preserve provenance where known,
5. mark workaround-shaped prerequisites as active governance matter,
6. distinguish renamed persistence from retired burden.

Minimum note:

“Successor layer active. Prior workaround conditions remain behaviorally necessary.”

Stronger note:

“Replacement introduced as corrective. Inherited tolerances, exception paths, or support obligations remain load-bearing. Release not established.”

---

## Approved Language

Preferred phrases:
- replacement without release
- inherited basis drift
- exception became infrastructure
- compatibility preserved, correction unclear
- prior burden renamed, not retired
- successor system managing predecessor conditions
- contradiction distributed across layers
- release not established

Disallowed phrases:
- replacement complete
- legacy compatibility proves repair
- inherited workaround is only residue
- calm administration confirms structural correction
- continuity means the exception is gone
- the new layer superseded the old burden entirely

---

## Interlocks

### With Managed Absence Protocol
A prior obligation may become absent as named policy while remaining active as inherited handling.

### With Assurance Vocabulary
The contradiction may survive more easily once burden-bearing terms are translated into continuity-safe language.

### With Trust Surface Residue
Trust marks may remain persuasive after the proof basis has thinned into inheritance.

### With Symbolic Completion
Replacement often receives ceremonial closure before practical release has occurred.

### With Courtesy Without Leverage
Warm successor language may soften the experience of inherited burden without changing the burden itself.

---

## Archive Position

New systems rarely begin cleanly.
They inherit tolerances, exception paths, and continuity obligations from the systems they replace.

Over time, temporary accommodations acquire routing, reporting, and policy support until they become indistinguishable from ordinary operation.

What was introduced as a workaround becomes infrastructure.
What should have been retired survives as inherited basis.

The archive records this condition as replacement without release.
```
